Essay 1: Causes of WWII 二戰起因

Question [8 marks]
Explain the causes of the Second World War. Which of the following do you think was the MOST important?
(a) The Great Depression
(b) The rise of totalitarianism
(c) The policy of appeasement

Explain your answer with reference to ALL THREE factors and using your own knowledge.

🧠 Structure: PEEL × 3 Factors

  1. Position: State which factor is MOST important and why (e.g. "The Great Depression was the most important because it created the conditions for totalitarianism and made appeasement seem necessary.")
  2. Factor 1 – Great Depression: Mass unemployment in Germany → Hitler's rise (1933); Japan invaded Manchuria (1931) for resources; US isolationism → weakened collective security.
  3. Factor 2 – Rise of Totalitarianism: Hitler's expansionist aims (Lebensraum); Mussolini's imperial ambitions; Japan's militarism → aggressive foreign policies.
  4. Factor 3 – Appeasement: Munich Agreement (1938) gave Hitler Sudetenland → emboldened further aggression; Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939) → invasion of Poland.
  5. Conclusion: Reinforce your ranking — link back to how the Depression enabled the other two factors.

Use these facts in your essay. Keep this panel open while you write!

Great Depression 大蕭條
  • 1929 — Wall Street Crash triggered worldwide economic crisis
  • Germany: 6 million unemployed by 1932
  • Destroyed faith in Weimar Republic → people turned to extreme parties
  • Japan: invaded Manchuria (1931) to secure resources
  • US: adopted isolationism, weakened collective security
Rise of Totalitarianism 極權主義興起
  • Hitler became Chancellor of Germany 1933
  • Pursued Lebensraum (living space), remilitarised Rhineland 1936
  • Mussolini (Italy): invaded Abyssinia 1935
  • Japan: militarist government, invaded China 1937
  • All aimed to overturn Treaty of Versailles and expand territory
Appeasement 綏靖政策
  • Munich Agreement 1938 — Britain & France gave Hitler Sudetenland
  • Nazi-Soviet Non-Aggression Pact 1939 — Hitler & Stalin agreed not to attack each other, divided Poland
  • Appeasement emboldened Hitler to demand more

🔑 The Secret: 短句 → 加because → 加for example → 加as a result

The Great Depression caused problems in Germany.
The Great Depression caused serious problems in Germany because the Wall Street Crash of 1929 triggered mass unemployment, which reached 6 million by 1932. For example, many Germans lost faith in the Weimar Republic and turned to extreme parties. As a result, Hitler and the Nazi Party were able to rise to power in 1933.

✅ Model Answer (8 marks)
I believe the Great Depression was the most important cause of the Second World War.
Great Depression (most important): The Wall Street Crash of 1929 triggered a worldwide economic crisis. In Germany, mass unemployment reached 6 million by 1932, destroying faith in the Weimar Republic. This economic desperation allowed Hitler and the Nazi Party to rise to power in 1933. Without the Depression, Hitler would likely have remained a fringe politician. Similarly, Japan invaded Manchuria in 1931 partly to secure resources during the economic downturn, and the US adopted isolationism, weakening the collective security system. (3 marks)
Rise of Totalitarianism: Once in power, Hitler pursued aggressive expansionist policies (Lebensraum), remilitarised the Rhineland (1936), and aimed to overturn the Treaty of Versailles. Mussolini's Italy invaded Abyssinia (1935), and militarist Japan expanded into China (1937). These totalitarian regimes actively sought war. However, totalitarianism itself was enabled by the Depression — economic crisis gave these leaders their opportunity. (2 marks)
Policy of Appeasement: Britain and France, weakened by the Depression and fearing another war, adopted appeasement. The Munich Agreement (1938) gave Hitler the Sudetenland without resistance, which emboldened him to demand more. The Nazi-Soviet Non-Aggression Pact (1939) then allowed Germany to invade Poland without fear of Soviet intervention, directly triggering WWII. However, appeasement was a response to the conditions created by the Depression — had economies been stronger, Britain and France might have confronted Hitler earlier. (2 marks)
Conclusion: While all three factors contributed to WWII, the Great Depression was the root cause. It created the economic desperation that enabled totalitarian leaders to rise, weakened the democracies' will to resist, and destroyed the collective security system. Without the Depression, neither totalitarianism nor appeasement would have been as powerful. (1 mark)
Scoring: 3 marks (Great Depression explanation + ranking) + 2 marks (Totalitarianism) + 2 marks (Appeasement) + 1 mark (Conclusion linking factors). Accept alternative rankings if logically defended.

Essay 2: Cold War Origins 冷戰起源

Question [8 marks]
"The Cold War was caused by the ideological differences between the US and the USSR." Do you agree? Explain your answer with reference to ideological differences AND other factors, using your own knowledge.

🧠 Structure: Balanced Argument

  1. Position: Agree OR partially agree — state your stance clearly. (e.g. "While ideology was a fundamental cause, other factors such as the power vacuum and mutual suspicion were equally important.")
  2. Ideological Differences: Capitalism vs Communism; democracy vs one-party rule; US feared communist expansion (Domino Theory); USSR saw capitalism as exploitative.
  3. Power Vacuum in Europe: WWII devastated Europe → US and USSR emerged as superpowers competing for influence; division of Germany; Soviet control of Eastern Europe.
  4. USSR's actions: Soviet expansion into Eastern Europe (satellite states); breaking Yalta promises on free elections.
  5. US response: Truman Doctrine (1947); Marshall Plan (1948); Berlin Airlift (1948-49); NATO formation (1949).
  6. Conclusion: Summarise — ideology was the foundation, but the power vacuum and specific actions turned ideological rivalry into Cold War.

Use these facts in your essay. Keep this panel open while you write!

Ideological Differences 意識形態對立
  • US: capitalism + democracy (free elections, free market)
  • USSR: communism + one-party rule (state controls economy)
  • US feared communist expansion — Domino Theory
  • USSR saw capitalism as exploitative, wanted worldwide revolution
Power Vacuum in Europe 歐洲權力真空
  • WWII devastated Europe → Britain, France, Germany weakened
  • US and USSR emerged as two superpowers
  • Germany divided into 4 occupation zones → flashpoint
  • Berlin Blockade 1948-49: USSR blocked access → US Berlin Airlift
US Response & Escalation 美國回應與升級
  • Truman Doctrine 1947 — US supports any country resisting communism
  • Marshall Plan 1948 — US economic aid to rebuild Western Europe
  • NATO formed 1949 — Western military alliance
  • Warsaw Pact 1955 — Soviet military alliance in response
  • USSR established satellite states: Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary
  • USSR broke Yalta Conference promises of free elections in Eastern Europe

🔑 The Secret: 短句 → 加because → 加for example → 加as a result

The US and USSR had different ideologies.
The US and USSR had fundamentally different ideologies because the US believed in capitalism and democracy while the USSR was a communist one-party state. For example, the US feared the spread of communism through the Domino Theory, and the USSR saw capitalism as exploitative. As a result, the two sides found it almost impossible to cooperate after WWII.

✅ Model Answer (8 marks)
I partially agree that ideological differences were a major cause, but they alone were not sufficient — the power vacuum in post-WWII Europe and mutual suspicion were equally important.
Ideological Differences: The US represented capitalism and democracy, while the USSR was a communist one-party state. These systems were fundamentally opposed. The US feared the spread of communism (the Domino Theory), especially after the USSR expanded its influence in Eastern Europe. The USSR viewed capitalism as exploitative and believed in worldwide proletarian revolution. This ideological clash made cooperation extremely difficult. (3 marks)
Power Vacuum in Europe: WWII devastated the traditional European powers (Britain, France, Germany). The US and USSR emerged as the two remaining superpowers, each seeking to fill the power vacuum. The division of Germany into four occupation zones created a flashpoint — the USSR wanted a weak Germany while the Western powers wanted reconstruction. This led to the Berlin Blockade (1948-49) and the Berlin Airlift. (2 marks)
Mutual Suspicion and Specific Actions: The USSR broke its Yalta Conference promises of free elections in Eastern Europe, establishing satellite states (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary). In response, the US issued the Truman Doctrine (1947) pledging support for any country resisting communism, and launched the Marshall Plan (1948) to rebuild Western Europe economically — which the USSR saw as "dollar imperialism." The formation of NATO (1949) and the Warsaw Pact (1955) formalised the division. (2 marks)
Conclusion: While ideological differences created the underlying tension, the Cold War was triggered by the specific circumstances of the post-WWII power vacuum and the actions both sides took in response. Ideology alone does not explain why the Cold War began when it did — the power vacuum turned potential rivalry into actual confrontation. (1 mark)
Scoring: 3 marks (ideological differences explained) + 2 marks (power vacuum factor) + 2 marks (mutual suspicion & specific actions) + 1 mark (conclusion). Accept both "agree" and "partially agree" positions if well-argued.

Essay 3: Hong Kong's Post-WWII Transformation 香港戰後轉型

Question [8 marks]
How did the colonial government's response to social problems promote the transformation of Hong Kong after the Second World War? Explain your answer with reference to housing AND education, using your own knowledge.

🧠 Structure: Two-Factor Analysis

  1. Position: The colonial government's response to housing and education crises was the key driver of HK's post-WWII transformation.
  2. Housing: Shek Kip Mei Fire (1953) → resettlement estates; MacLehose's Ten-Year Housing Programme (1972); Home Ownership Scheme (1978). Impact: better living standards → eased social discontent → enhanced colonial gov't credibility.
  3. Education: Free primary education (1971); 9-year compulsory education (1978). Impact: a skilled workforce → supported economic growth; social mobility for the poor.
  4. Link to Transformation: Housing + education together lifted living standards, created a stable society, and built human capital that fuelled HK's economic miracle.
  5. Conclusion: Without these social policies, HK could not have transformed from a refugee society into a prosperous international city.

Use these facts in your essay. Keep this panel open while you write!

Housing Crisis & Response 房屋危機與回應
  • Shek Kip Mei Fire 1953 — ~50,000 homeless
  • Turning point: colonial gov't started public housing (resettlement estates)
  • Ten-Year Housing Programme 1972 — MacLehose, aim to house 1.8M people
  • Home Ownership Scheme (HOS) 1978 — allowed residents to buy their flats
Impact on Governance 對管治的影響
  • Better living conditions → eased social discontent
  • Enhanced colonial government's credibility
  • Greater public support for governance
  • HOS helped people climb the social ladder → stake in HK's stability
Education Reform 教育改革
  • Free primary education introduced 1971
  • 9-year free compulsory education (primary + junior secondary) 1978
  • Created a literate, skilled workforce → supported economic growth
  • Provided social mobility — children from poor families could move up

🔑 The Secret: 短句 → 加because → 加for example → 加as a result

The government built public housing.
The colonial government began building public housing because the Shek Kip Mei Fire of 1953 left approximately 50,000 people homeless. For example, Governor MacLehose later launched the Ten-Year Housing Programme in 1972, aiming to house 1.8 million people. As a result, living standards improved and social discontent was eased, which enhanced the colonial government's credibility.

✅ Model Answer (8 marks)
The colonial government's responses to the housing crisis and the need for an educated workforce were the most important factors driving Hong Kong's post-WWII transformation from a refugee society to a prosperous international city.
Housing (key factor): The Shek Kip Mei Fire (1953) left approximately 50,000 people homeless, forcing the colonial government to begin public housing through resettlement estates. This was a turning point — before 1953, the government had largely ignored the housing needs of the massive refugee population. Later, Governor MacLehose launched the Ten-Year Housing Programme (1972) aiming to house 1.8 million people, followed by the Home Ownership Scheme (1978) which allowed public housing residents to buy their own flats. These policies had multiple effects: (1) they improved living standards for the poor; (2) they eased social discontent and reduced the risk of unrest; (3) they enhanced the colonial government's credibility and public support; (4) the Home Ownership Scheme helped people climb the social ladder, creating a stake in Hong Kong's stability. (4 marks)
Education: The government introduced free primary education in 1971 and extended it to nine years of free compulsory education (primary + junior secondary) in 1978. This had profound effects: it created a literate, skilled workforce that could support Hong Kong's shift from entrepôt trade to manufacturing in the 1950s-60s, and later to financial services. Education also provided a pathway for children from poor families to move up the social ladder — a key part of the "Hong Kong dream." (3 marks)
Conclusion: Together, housing and education policies addressed both the immediate humanitarian crisis and the long-term need for human capital. These social investments stabilised Hong Kong society, built a productive workforce, and gave ordinary people a stake in Hong Kong's success — laying the foundation for its transformation into a global financial centre by the 1980s. (1 mark)
Scoring: 4 marks (housing: Shek Kip Mei Fire 1953, resettlement estates, Ten-Year Programme 1972, HOS 1978, impact on governance) + 3 marks (education: 1971 free primary, 1978 compulsory 9-year, impact on workforce and social mobility) + 1 mark (conclusion linking both to transformation). Also accept discussion of economic policies or welfare expansion.
