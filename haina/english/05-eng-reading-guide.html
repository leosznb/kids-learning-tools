📖 Quick Strategy Reference ▾

3-Step Method: ① Read questions first → ② Skim passage → ③ Answer with precision
Main Idea: First + last paragraphs. Detail: Locate key word, read ±2 lines.
Inference: Combine clues, not stated directly. Reference: Look at the sentence before the pronoun.
Vocabulary: Read surrounding sentences. Tone: Focus on adjectives/adverbs.
⚠️ Traps: Near-identical but one word off · Extreme words (must/never/all) · True IRL but not in passage

[1]For most of human history, people slept when the sun went down and woke when it rose. But in the past century, artificial light, screens, and packed schedules have pushed bedtimes later and later. Today, the average teenager sleeps only 6.5 to 7 hours per night — far below the 8 to 10 hours recommended by doctors.

[2]Sleep is not simply "rest." During deep sleep, the brain clears out toxins, strengthens memories, and regulates emotions. Without enough deep sleep, teenagers become more irritable, find it harder to concentrate, and perform worse in exams — even if they have studied just as much.

[3]One notable culprit is the smartphone. The blue light emitted by screens tricks the brain into thinking it is still daytime, suppressing melatonin — the hormone that makes us feel sleepy. A 2023 study found that teenagers who used their phones within one hour of bedtime took an average of 45 minutes longer to fall asleep than those who put their devices away earlier.

[4]Schools around the world are beginning to respond. In California, a law now requires middle and high schools to start no earlier than 8:30 a.m. Early results show improved attendance and higher test scores. However, critics argue that later starts merely shift the problem rather than solving it, since teenagers simply stay up even later.

[5]The solution, most experts agree, lies in a combination of better habits and smarter policies. Putting away screens an hour before bed, keeping a consistent sleep schedule, and avoiding caffeine in the afternoon can make a remarkable difference. At the same time, schools and parents must recognise that sleep is not a luxury — it is a biological necessity.
