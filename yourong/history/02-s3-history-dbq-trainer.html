DBQ 1: Cold War — US-USSR Relations 冷戰美蘇關係（1959–1970s）

Based on 2024-25 S3 History 2nd Term Exam • Total: 12 marks

Source A
A report published on 25 July 1959 in a US newspaper. Khrushchev visited the American National Exhibition held in Moscow.
"Very refreshing!" was Mr. Khrushchev's comment after drinking several cups of cola during his visit. The Soviet leader walked to the display of the American kitchen, a model of what a typical working-class family would possess in America. While looking at the dishwasher and electric appliances displayed, he enthusiastically praised the exhibition as a sign of the progress in American technology.
Source B
A cartoon published in 1962 after the Cuban Missile Crisis. The man on the left represents the US leader (President Kennedy) and the man on the right represents the USSR leader (Khrushchev).
The cartoon shows the two leaders each sitting on large missiles pointing at each other. Between them is a trunk labelled "NUCLEAR WAR" with a monster peeking out. Kennedy says: "LET'S GET A LOCK FOR THIS THING." Both leaders are sweating nervously, trying to shut the trunk together.
(a)3
According to the US newspaper report, what was the attitude of Khrushchev towards the US commercial product mentioned in Source A? Explain your answer with reference to Source A.
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
Positive / Favourable attitude.
"Very refreshing!" was Mr. Khrushchev's comment after drinking several cups of cola. This shows he was satisfied with the taste of the cola and held a positive view of the American commercial product.
Scoring: 1 mark for attitude (Positive) + 1 mark for quoting "Very refreshing!" + 1 mark for explaining what it shows (satisfied/enjoyed)
(b)3
What was the form of conflict between the US and the USSR as shown in Source B? Explain your answer with reference to Source B.
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
Arms race / Military competition.
In Source B, both Kennedy and Khrushchev are sitting on large missiles pointing at each other. This symbolises that the US and USSR engaged in an intense arms race and military rivalry during the Cold War, each trying to out-arm the other. The trunk labelled "NUCLEAR WAR" further shows that this arms race threatened to escalate into nuclear conflict.
Scoring: 1 mark for form (arms race) + 1 mark for citing missiles in cartoon + 1 mark for explaining what it represents (military competition/confrontation)
(c)6
"In the 1950s–70s, the relationship between the US and the USSR experienced ups and downs." Do you agree? Explain your answer with reference to Sources A and B, and using your own knowledge.
✅ Model Answer (6 marks)
Agree.
Ups (improved relations): Source A shows in 1959, Khrushchev had a positive attitude towards American commercial products and praised US technology. This reflects a period of relatively improved relations—Khrushchev was open to American culture. (2 marks)

Downs (worsened relations): Source B, published in 1962 after the Cuban Missile Crisis, shows both leaders sitting on missiles and trying to lock away "nuclear war." This reflects that tensions had escalated to the point of potential nuclear confrontation—the most dangerous moment of the Cold War. (2 marks)

Own knowledge: After the Cuban Missile Crisis, both sides recognised the danger and took steps to reduce tensions. For example, the US and USSR held the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT) in the 1970s to limit nuclear weapons. This Détente period showed relations improved again. (2 marks)
Scoring: 2 marks (Source A interpretation) + 2 marks (Source B interpretation) + 2 marks (own knowledge). Accept both "agree" and "disagree" if logically argued.
💡 Strategy: For (c), always: position → Source A → Source B → own knowledge. Never give own knowledge without first citing the sources. 2+2+2 = 6 marks.

DBQ 2: Hong Kong — Industry & Housing (1960s–1970s) 香港工業與房屋

Based on 2024-25 S3 History 2nd Term Exam • Total: 12 marks

Source C
A cartoon drawn in the 1960s about a family using their bed spaces as a processing station for industrial production in Hong Kong.
The cartoon depicts a small flat where a family of six sits squeezed onto the bed spaces of one room. Each family member is engaged in different tasks: one is assembling plastic flowers, another sewing garments, a third packing toys. The bed itself serves as a workbench. Stacks of semi-finished products pile up in every corner. A factory owner stands at the door collecting the finished goods.
Source D
Adapted from a commentary about former Governor MacLehose (洪督麥理浩).
MacLehose launched the Ten-Year Housing Programme (1972) which allowed people living in small huts to move to public housing, and then those living in housing estates to purchase Home Ownership Scheme (1978) flats. Residents could gradually improve their lives and use this kind of housing as their "stepping stone" to buy larger flats and move to higher-income areas. MacLehose knew that there were a lot of poor people in Hong Kong, which would not be a problem as long as they had a chance to move upward in society.
(a)3
Infer from Source C ONE characteristic of Hong Kong industry in the 1960s. Explain your answer with reference to Source C.
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
Light industries / Labour-intensive industries prospered in 1960s Hong Kong.
In Source C, a family of six squeezes into the bed spaces of the same room and produces different light-industry products such as plastic flowers, garments and toys. The high demand for manual labour reflects the thriving development of labour-intensive light industries in Hong Kong at that time. The small-scale, home-based production also shows that many factories relied on cheap, flexible labour.
Scoring: 1 mark for identifying "light/labour-intensive industries" + 1 mark for citing evidence from Source C + 1 mark for explaining how the evidence supports the characteristic
(b)3
What was the impact of the implementation of various housing programmes on the governance of the colonial government of Hong Kong? Explain your answer with reference to Source D.
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
The housing programmes enhanced the effectiveness of governance.
In Source D, MacLehose stated: "there were a lot of poor people in Hong Kong, which would not be a problem as long as they had a chance to move upward in society." The housing programmes provided a better living environment and a chance for people to climb the social ladder, which helped to ease social discontent. As a result, the government's credibility among citizens would increase, leading to greater public support for colonial rule.
Scoring: 1 mark for impact (enhanced governance) + 1 mark for citing from Source D + 1 mark for explaining the causal link (better living → less discontent → more support)
(c)6
"The living standards of the people in Hong Kong had experienced a drastic change in the second half of the 20th century." Do you agree? Explain your answer with reference to Sources C and D, and using your own knowledge.
✅ Model Answer (6 marks)
Agree.
Before improvement (1960s): Source C shows that in the 1960s, a family of six squeezed into the bed spaces of one room, which was crowded and lacked privacy. They had to use their living space for work, reflecting poor living conditions and low living standards. (2 marks)

After improvement (1970s onwards): Source D shows the Ten-Year Housing Programme and Home Ownership Scheme provided better housing options for the grassroots class. People could use public housing as a "stepping stone" to buy larger flats, reflecting improved living standards. (2 marks)

Own knowledge: Hong Kong's economic transformation contributed significantly to this drastic change. Between the 1950s and 1990s, HK shifted from an entrepôt to a light-industry manufacturing centre, and eventually to an international financial centre. This economic prosperity allowed people to enjoy a higher standard of living, further supporting the claim of a drastic change in living conditions. (2 marks)
Scoring: 2 marks (Source C — poor conditions) + 2 marks (Source D — improvement through housing) + 2 marks (own knowledge — economic transformation). Accept both views if logically argued.
💡 Strategy: "Drastic change" = contrast between BEFORE and AFTER. Show the bad (Source C) → show the better (Source D) → explain WHY it got better (own knowledge: economic transformation).

DBQ 3: Cold War — The Marshall Plan 冷戰馬歇爾計劃

Adapted from 2023-24 S3 History 2nd Term Exam • Total: 13 marks • ⚠️ Sources reconstructed from OCR; historical content accurate, wording may differ from original exam

Source A — Extract 1
A speech by a US official in 1947 promoting the Marshall Plan.
"Our policy is directed not against any country or doctrine but against hunger, poverty, desperation and chaos. Its purpose should be the revival of a working economy in the world so as to permit the emergence of political and social conditions in which free institutions can exist. The Marshall Plan is a gesture of American goodwill to help our European friends rebuild their shattered economies after the devastation of war."
Source A — Extract 2
A statement from a Soviet newspaper, Pravda, 1947, criticising the Marshall Plan.
"The Marshall Plan is nothing but a scheme of American economic imperialism. By offering 'aid,' the United States seeks to control the economies of European nations, making them dependent on Washington. The real aim is to create a bloc of states bound by obligations to the US, and to use this bloc as a tool against the Soviet Union. It is a plan for interference in the internal affairs of other countries."
Source B
A Soviet propaganda poster published c. 1949. The poster caption labels the Marshall Plan as the "Marshall-Stalin Plan."
The poster depicts Uncle Sam (representing the US) handing over bags of money labelled "Marshall Plan Aid" to Western European countries. Behind Uncle Sam stands the shadow of Joseph Stalin, the Soviet leader, pulling the strings of Uncle Sam like a puppet master. The caption reads: "The Marshall-'Stalin' Plan — Two Sides of the Same Coin." The implication is that the US, like the USSR, uses economic control to dominate other nations — thereby discrediting American aid by equating it with Soviet-style domination.
(a)4
Do Extract 1 and Extract 2 in Source A have a similar view towards the motive of the Marshall Plan? Cite 1 clue from Extract 1 and 1 clue from Extract 2 to explain your answer.
✅ Model Answer (4 marks)
No, they have opposite views.
Extract 1 (US view): The Marshall Plan aims at "revival of a working economy" and is described as "American goodwill to help European friends rebuild." → The US presents it as a humanitarian and economic recovery programme. (2 marks)

Extract 2 (Soviet view): The Marshall Plan is described as "American economic imperialism" that seeks to "control the economies of European nations" and create "a tool against the Soviet Union." → The USSR sees it as a political weapon for US domination. (2 marks)
Scoring: 1 mark for "No/opposite views" + 1 mark for Extract 1 quote & explanation + 1 mark for Extract 2 quote & explanation + 1 mark for contrasting analysis
(b)3
Infer the purpose of the Soviet propaganda shown in Source B. Explain your answer with reference to Source B.
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
To discredit the Marshall Plan by equating it with Soviet-style control.
By renaming it the "Marshall-Stalin Plan," the Soviet propaganda poster implies that the US, like the USSR, uses economic aid as a tool to control and dominate other nations. The image of Stalin pulling Uncle Sam's strings suggests both superpowers are equally imperialist. The purpose is to undermine Western European trust in US aid and to present the USSR's criticism as justified—"both sides are the same."
Scoring: 1 mark for purpose (discredit/undermine US aid) + 1 mark for citing specific element from Source B + 1 mark for explaining how the propaganda works
(c)6
Which country, the United States or the USSR, should be more responsible for the tensions between them during the Cold War period? Explain your answer with reference to Sources A and B, and using your own knowledge.
✅ Model Answer (6 marks)
Both the US and USSR shared responsibility, but arguments can be made for either side. Marks are awarded for logical reasoning with source evidence and own knowledge.
US responsibility: Source A Extract 1 shows the US claimed the Marshall Plan was purely for economic recovery, but the Soviet view in Extract 2 saw it as "American economic imperialism" and a tool against the USSR. Own knowledge: the US adopted the Truman Doctrine (1947) to contain communism globally, established NATO (1949) as an anti-Soviet military alliance, and engaged in an arms race.

USSR responsibility: Source B shows the USSR actively spreading anti-US propaganda to undermine Western unity. Own knowledge: the USSR expanded communist control over Eastern European satellite states (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary), initiated the Berlin Blockade (1948-49), formed the Warsaw Pact (1955), and placed nuclear missiles in Cuba leading to the Cuban Missile Crisis (1962).

Balanced view: Both sides escalated tensions—the US through containment and military alliances, and the USSR through expansion and proxy conflicts. The Cold War was a product of mutual suspicion rather than one side's aggression alone.
Scoring: 2 marks (Source A interpretation: either US or USSR actions) + 2 marks (Source B: Soviet propaganda) + 2 marks (own knowledge examples). Any logically supported position is accepted — the key is using BOTH sources AND own knowledge.
💡 Strategy: For "which is more responsible" questions, the exam DOES NOT expect a single correct answer. Focus on balanced argument — show evidence from BOTH sides, then state your own reasoned conclusion. You can argue "both equally" if well-supported.
